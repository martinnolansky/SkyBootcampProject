What is Agile?

“Agile” describes a way of working where self-organizing and cross-functional teams can develop evolving solutions which adapt to changing environments, requirements, and new learnings and ideas. The Agile methodology was formalized in 2001 with the Agile Manifesto. But a lot of the Agile concepts are not new! In fact, most of us have probably applied it in some way when doing high school science projects. Twelve principles based on the manifesto were created as a guideline to help development teams follow the agile methodology. These include customer satisfaction, changing requirements, frequent delivery, regular communication, supporting team members, face-to-face communication, measure work progress, development process, good design, measure progress, continue to seek results as well as reflecting and adapting frequently. So, why might we use Agile? As humans, we're used to sticking with rigid structures and linear steps. But in most projects or processes, things are likely to change in ways we didn't consider or know about when we first started. With agile methodology, we emphasize adaptability and incremental improvements to our solution. So, if our business is disrupted by external or internal factors, we're better able to respond. There exists different agile methodologies such as Scrum and DSDM.

Scrum

Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps people, teams, and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems. In a Scrum team, there are 3 key roles which include the product owner who maximizes value from the product, the Scrum master who is a servant leader to the team and the developers who self-organize and collectively develop the software or product. To understand the Scrum methodology, it is important to understand the underlying theory, main artifacts, and events which create the Scrum methodology.

Theory
Incremental Delivery: Scrum focuses on delivering value in small, frequent increments. This approach allows teams to provide tangible results to stakeholders at the end of each sprint, fostering a steady stream of progress.

Empiricism: The decisions in Scrum are based on empirical evidence and experience. The team makes informed choices by observing and measuring their work and its outcomes. This data-driven approach encourages informed decision-making rather than relying solely on predictions.

Transparency: Openness and visibility are vital in Scrum. The team, stakeholders, and other relevant parties share information about the work, progress, and challenges. This transparency fosters trust and collaboration among all involved.

Inspection: Regularly examining the work, processes, and outcomes is a central aspect of Scrum. This inspection helps identify potential issues early on, allowing the team to make necessary adjustments and improvements.

Adaptation: Scrum embraces change and encourages teams to adapt based on new information. If issues arise or circumstances change, the team can adjust their plans, processes, and goals to better address the situation.


3 main Artifacts - to help manage work
Product Backlog: This is an ordered list of all the desired features, enhancements, bug fixes, and other work items that provide value to the product. The Product Owner is responsible for maintaining and prioritizing this backlog based on input from stakeholders and the team.

Sprint Backlog: For each sprint, the team selects a set of items from the Product Backlog that they commit to completing. The Sprint Backlog outlines the specific tasks and activities required to turn the selected backlog items into a shippable product increment.

Increment: At the end of each sprint, the team delivers a potentially shippable product version. This increment includes all the work completed during the sprint, adhering to the Definition of Done (DoD). The increment is a tangible result that adds value to the product and could potentially be released to users.



5 main Events
Sprint Planning: At the start of each sprint, the team holds a Sprint Planning meeting to determine which backlog items they will work on during the sprint. The team also defines the goals and objectives for the sprint.

The Sprint: Contains all of the other events and timeboxed to a maximum of 4 weeks. The aim of the sprint is to deliver a completed, usable increment which meets the sprint goal.

Daily Scrum: Also known as the daily stand-up, this is a short daily meeting where the team members synchronize their work. They discuss progress, challenges, and plans for the day. It's an opportunity to foster communication and collaboration.

Sprint Review: At the end of each sprint, the team demonstrates the work they've completed to stakeholders. Feedback is collected, and the Product Backlog is adjusted based on this input. The team also reflects on their performance.

Sprint Retrospective: This event occurs after the Sprint Review and focuses on the team's process. The team discusses what went well, what could be improved, and formulates action items for refining their processes in the next sprint.

Agile Methodologies - DSDM

What is DSDM?
Dynamic System Development Method (DSDM) is an agile project method that focuses on the overall project lifecycle. We can use it to help produce quick and efficient results for the organization. DSDM has 8 principles: focus on the business needs, develop iteratively, demonstrate control, deliver on time, build incrementally from firm foundations, communicate continuously and clearly, collaborate and never compromise quality.



Roles and Responsibilities
There are various roles and responsibilities within the DSDM framework. Every role is an important player in DSDM. We only discuss the key project level and Solution Development Team (SDT) roles here.

Business sponsor: senior project level role (AKA the project champion), is responsible for the business case and project budget/finance.

Business visionary: provides a clear vision, communicates business sponsor's needs, and ensures these are represented within the business case. Also makes sure the solution adds value to the overall business.

Business ambassador: responsible for daily communication between the project and the business. They are the key business representative within the Solution Development Team (SDT).

Project manager: co-ordinates high level management aspects of the project, focuses on managing the working environment for the evolving solution.

Technical coordinator: responsible for authority of the project, ensures project is technically coherent and meets agreed standards as well as provides the technical vision.

Team leader: leads the SDT, ensuring the team works together efficiently to fulfill stated objectives. Also involved in planning and coordination of product delivery.

Solution developer: comprehends the business requirements and converts them into a deployable solution.

Solution tester: conducts testing in compliance with the agreed strategy.


DSDM Process
The DSDM process is used to create the project lifecycle. DSDM provides an iterative and incremental approach to development and delivery. We primarily focus on the most vital business needs, with less crucial features being delivered later. This approach allows business representatives to view the solution as it evolves, provide regular feedback, and request changes throughout the solution development process. There are two types of products which are evolutionary and milestone products. There are various products including the feasibility assessment, business case, and the prioritized requirement list.

Requirements Gathering

Requirements refer to the detailed descriptions of the capabilities, features, functionalities, and qualities that a business solution must possess to meet the needs and goals of the stakeholders. These are usually gathered by a business analyst who must consider the requirements of many business areas.

Business requirements represent the high-level needs and objectives of the organization, describing the larger goals, problems, or opportunities that drive the need for a solution. Stakeholder requirements capture the needs, expectations, and preferences of various stakeholders who will interact with or be affected by the solution. This includes customers, users, local communities, and regulatory bodies.

After gathering all of the requirements, requirements can be broken down into either functional or non-functional requirements. Functional requirements are requirements which detail what the system should do whereas non-functional requirements detail how the system executes a specific function. MOSCOW Prioritisation

Dynamic System Development Method (DSDM) is an agile project method that focuses on the overall project lifecycle. We can use it to help produce quick and efficient results for the organization. One of DSDM practices is MoSCoW.

MoSCoW is a prioritization technique used to help comprehend and manage priorities in order to track progress and meet deadlines. The technique can be used for requirements and user stories.

The capitalized letters represent the level of priority:

Must have (Minimum Usable SubseT (MUST) of requirements the project assures to deliver).

Should have (Important but not crucial).

Could have (Advantageous but if left out has minimal impact).

Won't have (Will not be delivered in this timeframe).

How did we use MOSCOW?

To demonstrate how MoSCoW is used, we used MoSCoW for this project! Please refer to the image in the top right for our MoSCoW prioritization. Firstly, we discussed and recorded the requirements for the project. We then collated and inserted all of the requirements into an online tool called 'ClickUp', in which we then collectively discussed each requirement and its level of prioritization of being either a must-have, should-have, could-have, or won't have at present.

Upon successfully allocating each requirement's priority level, we then started working on the 'must-have' requirements column, with specific requirements being assigned to each team member(s). After each increment, we as a team reviewed the requirements once again, re-adjusting priority levels where necessary.

Project Planning & Kanban Board

Kanban Boards
Progress tracking is essential to work more agilely. This guide will explore Kanban Boards' methods for monitoring progress in an agile environment. Derived from the Toyota Production System, Kanban boards are a versatile tool for monitoring progress in agile settings. Following these steps:

Visualize Workflow: Create columns such as "To Do," "In Progress," "In Test," and "Done."

Limit Work in Progress: Focus on tasks you can manage effectively at any given time.

Manage Flow: Ensure that work items move through the system without blockages.

Measure and Iterate: Continuously assess the flow, making incremental improvements.

Explicit Process Policies: Define and communicate how tasks are handled.

Improve Collaboratively: Foster a team-wide commitment to improvement.



Kanban Principles and Practices:
Principles: Start with What You Do Now: Map existing tasks onto the Kanban board. Incremental, Evolutionary Change: Implement small changes over time and monitor their effects. Respect the Current Process: Acknowledge existing processes, roles, and titles. Evolve them gradually. Encourage Acts of Leadership: Every team member can display leadership.

Practices: Visualize Workflow: Populate the Kanban board with tasks to visually represent your workflow. Limit Work in Progress: Focus on completing tasks before moving to new ones to minimize distractions. Manage Flow: Ensure a steady flow of tasks through the system, making adjustments as needed. Make Process Policies Explicit: Clearly define how tasks are approached and maintain visibility. Improve Collaboratively: Enhance processes collectively to maximize the team's efficiency and effectiveness.

Kanban Boards offer insightful methods to gauge progress in agile environments and facilitate continuous workflow visualization and improvement. Incorporate these tools to elevate your agile practices and keep your projects on track.



Project planning
To track our project progress effectively, we employed a Kanban board. This visual management tool allowed us to monitor the status of each task throughout the project lifecycle. The board was divided into columns representing different stages of work, such as "To Do," "In Progress," "In Review," and "Done." Team members could easily move tasks between columns as they progressed. Limiting work in progress helped maintain focus and ensured that tasks were completed before new ones were started. Regular reviews and retrospectives allowed us to adapt and improve our workflow continuously.
