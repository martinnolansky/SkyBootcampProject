What is Business Analysis?

Business analysis is the practice of identifying business needs and finding solutions to business problems. It involves understanding the structure, processes, policies, and strategies of an organization and using that knowledge to recommend processes or organizational improvements to achieve strategic goals. A business analyst has responsibility for many tasks including:

  • Requirements Gathering
    Business analysts work closely with stakeholders to elicit, document, and analyze their requirements. This involves understanding the needs and expectations of various stakeholders, including customers, users, and internal teams.

  • Process Analysis
    Business analysts examine current business processes to identify inefficiencies, bottlenecks, and areas for improvement. They map out workflows, analyze data flows, and identify opportunities to optimize processes.

  • Solution Design
    Based on the gathered requirements and process analysis, business analysts collaborate with other teams to design solutions that address the identified needs. This may involve suggesting changes to existing processes, developing new software applications, or implementing new technologies.

  • Stakeholder Communication
    Effective communication is crucial in business analysis. Business analysts act as intermediaries between technical teams and non-technical stakeholders. They ensure that everyone involved has a clear understanding of the project's goals, progress, and outcomes.

Requirements Gathering

What are requirements - Requirements refer to the detailed descriptions of the capabilities, features, functionalities, and qualities that a business solution must possess to meet the needs and goals of the stakeholders. These are usually gathered by a business analyst who must consider the requirements of many business areas. Business requirements represent the high-level needs and objectives of the organization, describing the larger goals, problems, or opportunities that drive the need for a solution. Stakeholder requirements capture the needs, expectations, and preferences of various stakeholders who will interact with or be affected by the solution. This includes customers, users, local communities, and regulatory bodies.

You must interview stakeholders to build a rapport with the user community, gathering information to understand the area of the business under investigation with the aim of gathering requirements for the project. This is usually done by creating a \‘terms of reference\’ and coverage matrix to help determine the questioning strategy (where to use open and closed questions). This will help you to structure the interview, making sure the most important topics are first and sensitive topics come later in the interview after a rapport has been built.

Functional & Non-functional Requirements

Functional requirements describe the specific functionalities or features that a system must provide. They define the interactions between the system and its users, as well as the system's behavior under different conditions. Functional requirements answer the question, "What should the system do?". These are often summarized as CRUD (Create, Read, Update, & Delete).

Non-functional requirements, sometimes referred to as quality attributes or performance attributes, focus on the qualities and constraints that the system should exhibit. These requirements are not directly related to specific functionalities but rather describe how well the system performs those functionalities. Non-functional requirements answer the question, "How should the system perform?"

Requirement Filtering

Many problems can arise when gathering requirements as they can be difficult to write in a well-structured way. Some of these issues include: Requirements that are not well defined, Ambiguous requirements, Contradiction between requirements, Overlapping or duplicate requirements, Requirements that are not testable. To avoid the issues mentioned above, requirements must follow the SMART structure. In doing so, we ensure the requirements are clear, cohesive, understandable, and unambiguous.
