What is Business Analysis?

Business analysis is the practice of identifying business needs and finding solutions to business problems. It involves understanding the structure, processes, policies, and strategies of an organization to recommend processes or organizational improvements to achieve strategic goals. A business analyst has responsibility for many tasks including:

  • Requirements Gathering
    BAs work closely with stakeholders to elicit, document, and analyze their requirements. This work involves understanding the needs and expectations of various stakeholders, including customers, users, and internal teams.

  • Process Analysis
    BAs examine current business processes to identify inefficiencies, bottlenecks, and areas for improvement. They map out workflows, analyze data flows, and identify opportunities to optimize processes.

  • Solution Design
    Based on the gathered requirements and process analysis, BAs collaborate with other teams to design solutions that address the identified needs. This work may involve suggesting changes to existing processes, developing new software applications, or implementing new technologies.

  • Stakeholder Communication
    Effective communication is crucial in business analysis. BAs act as intermediaries between technical teams and non-technical stakeholders. They ensure that everyone involved has a clear understanding of the project goals, progress and outcomes.

Terms of Reference

A terms of reference (TOR) is needed to eliminate any misunderstanding between the project’s stakeholders, it is important to ensure that any substantial piece of work has terms of reference. In the form of a BOSCARD template document, there are seven different sections that need to be observed to achieve project success.

The TOR is a crucial step in the planning and evaluation of any project. It is often referred to as the “contract” and must be agreed to by all stakeholders. Therefore, we need to understand:

What the objectives are and how success is measured?
What the scope of the work is?
What the deliverables are and how they will be reviewed and accepted?
Who else is involved and what are their roles and responsibilities?

Requirements Gathering

Requirements refer to the detailed descriptions of the capabilities, features, functionalities, and qualities that a business solution must possess to meet the needs and goals of the stakeholders. These requirements are usually gathered by a BA. Business requirements represent the high-level needs and objectives of the organization, describing the larger goals, problems, or opportunities that drive the need for a solution. Stakeholder requirements capture the needs, expectations, and preferences of various stakeholders who will interact with or be affected by the solution. The requirements cover customers, users, local communities, and regulatory bodies.

The stakeholders must be interviewed to build a rapport with the user community, gathering information to understand the area of the business under investigation. Building a coverage matrix will help to determine the questioning strategy (where to use open and closed questions) and will help you to structure the interview, ensuring that the most important topics are first and sensitive topics come later in the interview after a rapport has been built.

Functional & Non-functional Requirements

Functional requirements describe the specific functionalities or features that a system must provide. They define the interactions between the system and its users, as well as the system's behavior under different conditions. Functional requirements answer the question, "What should the system do?". These are often summarized as CRUD (Create, Read, Update, & Delete).

Non-functional requirements, sometimes referred to as quality or performance attributes, focus on the qualities and constraints that the system should exhibit. These requirements are not directly related to specific functionalities but rather describe how well the system performs those functionalities. Non-functional requirements determine how the should the system perform.

Requirement Filtering

Many problems can arise when gathering requirements as they can be difficult to write in a well-structured way. Some of these issues include: Requirements that are not well-defined, ambiguous, contradictory, overlapping or duplicate requirements, and those that are not testable. To avoid the issues mentioned above, requirements must follow the SMART structure. In doing so, we ensure the requirements are clear, cohesive, understandable, and unambiguous.

Stakeholders

Stakeholders are individuals, groups, or entities that have a vested interest in the outcome of a business analysis effort. They can influence or are influenced by the decisions, actions, and outcomes of a business analysis initiative.

Identifying and involving stakeholders is a crucial aspect of successful business analysis, as their needs, perspectives, and requirements must be considered to ensure that the analysis aligns with business goals and objectives. BAs must identify, engage, and manage these stakeholders throughout the analysis process. Effective communication, collaboration, and understanding of their needs and perspectives are critical to ensuring the success of the initiative.

There are two categories of stakeholders: internal and external stakeholders. The distinction between internal and external stakeholders is based on their relationship to the organization.
Internal stakeholders are individuals or groups who are directly associated with the organization and have a close operational or financial connection to it. They are typically part of the organization's structure and have a direct influence on its operations and decisions.

External stakeholders are individuals or groups who are not directly employed by the organization but still have a vested interest in its activities and outcomes. They can influence or be influenced by the organization's actions, and their perspectives need to be considered in decision-making.


Prioritising Stakeholders

Prioritizing stakeholders involves identifying and ranking stakeholders based on their importance, influence, and impact on a project, initiative, or organization. Effective stakeholder prioritization helps ensure that resources and efforts are focused on those stakeholders who have the greatest potential to influence the project's success or who are most affected by its outcomes.

These priorities can be mapped out using a power-interest grid. This is a visual tool used in project management and stakeholder analysis to help classify stakeholders based on their level of power or influence and their level of interest in a project or initiative. This matrix is useful for determining how to effectively engage and manage stakeholders to ensure the success of a project or decision.

User Personas, Stories & Wireframes

User personas are fictional characters representing different user types. They embody characteristics, goals, and behaviors of real users. Personas help teams understand user diversity and design with specific users in mind. This promotes user-centered development and tailors solutions to different needs.

User stories are concise descriptions of features, expressed from the user's perspective. Following the format "As a [type of user], I want to [perform an action] so that [achieve a goal]," they capture user needs and expectations. These stories guide agile development, ensuring solutions align with user requirements.

Wireframes are visual blueprints of digital interfaces to illustrate layout and structure. They provide an early-stage visual guide for designers, developers, and stakeholders. Wireframes focus on arrangement and functionality, aiding conceptualization and alignment before detailed design and coding begin.
